\begin{abstract}
ADHD is commonly framed through hyperactivity, impulsivity, inattention, and executive-function difficulties, while its associated cognitive strengths receive less attention. Existing task management applications often rely on reminders, timers, and behavioural correction, potentially reinforcing deficit-orientated assumptions.

This paper presents StrengthFlow, a strength-based task management application for adults with ADHD. It translates six ADHD-related cognitive strengths into design principles addressing challenges in activation, concentration, effort, emotion, memory, and task execution. A high-fidelity prototype was preliminarily evaluated with three adults with ADHD. Feedback indicated positive responses to its strength-based framing and visual design, while highlighting the need to better support use in distracting environments. StrengthFlow demonstrates how neurodiversity-informed design can promote capability, autonomy, and positive identity, reflecting the principle that “people with differences may not need a cure; they need help and accommodation.”

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Behavioural characteristics of ADHD do not exist in a binary form of normal and
abnormal~\cite{sedgwick2019}. A person with ADHD experiences deficits in
cognitive control, sensitivity to rewards, and time perception, while
simultaneously displaying high energy, creativity, hyperfocus, empathy, and
willingness to help others. The core deficit is \textbf{executive dysfunction}
\cite{nigg2005}---a broad range of top-down cognitive processes and abilities
that enable flexible, goal-directed behaviour~\cite{miyake2000}---which
significantly affects the daily social activities of adults with ADHD.

Current approaches to alleviating executive symptoms include traditional
medications---whose global market continues to grow~\cite{scheffler2007}---and
behavioural therapies; however, they cannot maintain long-term symptom relief
and have severe limitations in terms of side effects~\cite{enriquez2019},
making psychosocial interventions a necessary complement~\cite{safren2006}. One widely adapted psychosocial tool is the
digital application. A systematic review of 355 app-related studies found that
most effectiveness outcomes were negative~\cite{pasarelu2020}: poor outcomes
were not maintained long after the intervention~\cite{gajecki2014}, and users
reported more severe anxiety when separated from the tool~\cite{torous2016}.
More critically, these control-oriented tools reinforce the negative mental
model that ``I need to be managed,'' undermining self-esteem.

Brown's model of ADHD executive functioning identifies six impairment
dimensions---activation, concentration, effort, emotion, memory, and
execution. A complementary qualitative study defines six positive core themes in
ADHD: \textbf{cognitive vitality}, \textbf{courage}, \textbf{energy},
\textbf{humanity}, \textbf{resilience}, and \textbf{transcendence}
\cite{schippers2022}. StrengthFlow maps each cognitive strength to its
corresponding executive challenge and implements an interaction module that
activates the strength to support task engagement.

\section{User Needs and Gaps in Existing ADHD Tools}

Approximately 2.8\% of adults worldwide have ADHD~\cite{faraone2006}. An
analysis of two qualitative studies (422 adults with ADHD across six countries)
showed that participants described their own strengths---problem-solving,
fantasy, novelty-seeking, deep focus, empathy, resilience---yet received little
external recognition of them. A review of 127 ADHD studies found that 29\% of
treated and 56\% of untreated adults reported low self-esteem, and 38--73\%
felt their social functioning was affected~\cite{harpin2016}. These findings
point to two parallel needs: (1)~social recognition and affirmation of ADHD
strengths; and (2)~support for executive skill and self-esteem through positive
engagement rather than coercion.

Existing digital tools have attempted to address these needs, but predominantly through a deficit-correction lens. We examined a prior systematic review of 22 representative ADHD management applications (10,000--50,000 downloads) covering task management, habit building, time management, focus enhancement, emotion regulation, and memory support~\cite{pasarelu2020}, and applied a supplementary strength-based analysis classifying each module by whether its interaction design leverages ADHD cognitive strengths. Table~\ref{tab:apps} presents the results. Most modules rely on mandatory reminders, countdown timers, and forced behavioural prompts, with no leverage of cognitive strengths. A subset---visual mapping, meditation, community interaction, and random creative prompts---does align with positive ADHD characteristics. However, no existing tool integrates these strength-aligned elements into a unified, theoretically grounded framework. This gap---between what users need (strength affirmation and positive engagement) and what current tools offer (deficit correction)---motivates the research presented here.

\section{Neurodiversity Framework: Strengths and Executive Challenges}

The neurodiversity of executive functions in ADHD is concentrated in four brain
regions: the prefrontal cortex, the basal ganglia, the cerebellum, and the
parietal-temporal region, operating through the reticular activating
system~\cite{castellanos2002,valera2007,nakao2011,durston2004}. These regions
cannot be categorised independently; they form a dynamic, integrated management
process in which executive processing depends on coordinated activity across
multiple cognitive components.

A fundamental assumption of the neurodiversity paradigm is that all forms of
neurological variation are valuable~\cite{denhouting2019}. Within this paradigm,
qualitative research by Sedgwick et al.~\cite{sedgwick2019} and Schippers
et al.~\cite{schippers2022} identifies six core positive themes in adults with
ADHD: \textbf{cognitive vitality}, \textbf{courage}, \textbf{energy},
\textbf{humanity}, \textbf{resilience}, and \textbf{transcendence}. Brown’s
executive functioning model~\cite{nigg2005} identifies six corresponding
impairment dimensions: \textbf{activation}, \textbf{concentration},
\textbf{effort}, \textbf{emotion}, \textbf{memory}, and \textbf{execution}.
The six dimensions within this model map one-to-one onto the six positive
themes, a structural correspondence arising from shared neural substrates: the
same brain regions and networks that produce each executive impairment also
generate the corresponding cognitive
strength~\cite{castellanos2002,valera2007,nakao2011,durston2004}. Each
strength is therefore a principled, neurologically grounded compensatory
advantage for its paired deficit. The six pairings and their neural bases are
as follows.

\textbf{Cognitive Vitality vs.\ Concentration.}
Cognitive vitality describes the continuous mental activity, spontaneous
non-sequential thinking, vivid imagery, and intense internal focus often
associated with ADHD~\cite{stine2008,ammaniti2013}. These characteristics may
be linked to the default mode network (DMN), which supports internally oriented
processes such as self-reflection, memory, imagination, and future thinking, but
is typically suppressed during externally focused tasks~\cite{sestieri2011}.
From a neurodiversity perspective, altered DMN dynamics---particularly reduced
suppression during external tasks---can be understood as having two interrelated
aspects: they may support creativity, curiosity, divergent thinking, and rapid
associations, while also competing with the attention required to sustain and
complete external tasks. \textbf{Design implication:} rather than suppressing
internal ideation, design can use these cognitive strengths as entry points for
redirecting attention towards externally required tasks~\cite{mole2022}.

\textbf{Courage vs.\ Emotion.}
Courage describes how some people with ADHD confront fear and uncertainty
through non-conformity, risk-taking, integrity, persistence, and
self-determination~\cite{williams2008}. These characteristics may be associated
with differences in the amygdala and ventral striatum, which contribute to
emotional processing, reward anticipation, and behavioural
motivation~\cite{plessen2006,plichta2014}. From a neurodiversity perspective,
this variation in emotion and reward processing can be understood as having two
interrelated aspects: heightened sensitivity to negative stimuli and reduced
responsiveness to delayed rewards may contribute to emotional reactivity,
impulsivity, and avoidance of distant outcomes, while responsiveness to
immediate or personally meaningful rewards may support rapid decision-making,
persistence, and willingness to act~\cite{du2006,knutson2001}.
\textbf{Design implication:} design can channel courage through immediate,
positively framed actions and short-term rewards to reduce procrastination,
while recognising that these strategies do not directly address emotional
outbursts.

\textbf{Energy vs.\ Activation.}
Energy describes the willpower and sustained drive that some people with ADHD
experience when pursuing personally meaningful goals, often accompanied by a
strong sense of purpose~\cite{schippers2022}. These characteristics may be
associated with differences in the balance between the dorsal attention network
(DAN), which supports goal-directed attention, and the ventral attention network
(VAN), which supports the flexible reorientation of
attention~\cite{corbetta2002}. From a neurodiversity perspective, this
variation in attention regulation can be understood as having two interrelated
aspects: it may enable intense engagement and hyperfocus when a task is
personally meaningful, while also making it difficult to shift attention,
initiate less stimulating tasks, and organise competing priorities.
\textbf{Design implication:} design can connect required tasks to personally
meaningful goals, using purpose and intrinsic motivation to support task
activation and prioritisation.

\textbf{Humanity vs.\ Execution.}
In this framework, humanity describes a positive core theme reported by some
people with ADHD, encompassing social intelligence, humour, self-acceptance,
empathy, and emotional awareness in social interactions. These characteristics
may involve social-cognitive and affective processes associated with the mirror
neuron system, which supports the perception and internal representation of
others’ actions and emotions~\cite{humphrey1976,salovey1990}. From a
neurodiversity perspective, variation in social and emotional processing among
some people with ADHD can be understood as having two interrelated aspects: it
may support strong empathy, positive emotional expression, and social
connection, while also contributing to difficulties interpreting others’
responses and adapting behaviour when an interaction becomes uncertain or
negative. \textbf{Design implication:} design can use supportive social
interaction and positive emotional expression to sustain engagement, while
recognising that additional guidance may be needed to support behavioural
adaptation.

\textbf{Resilience vs.\ Effort.}
In this framework, resilience describes a positive core theme reported by some
people with ADHD, encompassing self-regulation, self-protection, and behavioural
adaptation under adverse conditions~\cite{garmezy1971}. These processes may
involve the anterior cingulate cortex, which contributes to cognitive control,
conflict monitoring, and behavioural regulation~\cite{carter2007}. From a
neurodiversity perspective, variation in effort regulation among some people
with ADHD can be understood as having two interrelated aspects: it may support
adaptive coping and behavioural adjustment in response to adversity, while
sustained demands for self-control may also lead to mental fatigue and reduced
capacity to maintain effort, described in this literature as ego
depletion~\cite{baumeister2000}. \textbf{Design implication:} design should
support resilience through flexible and optional coping strategies integrated
with other forms of task support, rather than treating self-regulation as a
stand-alone or compulsory solution.

\textbf{Transcendence vs.\ Memory.}
In this framework, transcendence describes a positive core theme reported by
some people with ADHD, encompassing intense appreciation of beauty and
excellence, particularly in music and other artistic experiences, together with
feelings of awe, wonder, and deep absorption~\cite{martinez2016,shiota2006}.
These characteristics may involve working-memory processes associated with
corticocerebellar networks and noradrenergic modulation. From a neurodiversity
perspective, variation in memory and experiential processing among some people
with ADHD can be understood as having two interrelated aspects: emotionally and
aesthetically meaningful experiences may promote deep engagement and memorable
encoding, while information lacking personal, emotional, or sensory salience may
be more difficult to retain and recall. \textbf{Design implication:} design can
use aesthetically engaging and personally meaningful representations to support
the encoding and recall of task-related information, while recognising that
aesthetic engagement alone may not address all working-memory difficulties.

Across all six pairings, a consistent finding emerges: the same neural systems
that produce each executive impairment also underlie the corresponding cognitive
strength. This shared neural substrate means that each strength is not merely a
compensatory workaround but a direct, neurologically grounded lever for
addressing its paired challenge. Divergent thinking and concentration share the
DMN; courage and emotional reactivity share the amygdala--ventral striatum
system; energy and activation share the DAN--VAN balance; humanity and
execution share mirror neuron processes; resilience and effort share the
anterior cingulate cortex; and transcendence and memory share
corticocerebellar--noradrenergic networks. Together, these pairings provide a
principled theoretical basis---grounded in neurodiversity research rather than
deficit assumptions---from which a concrete design direction can be derived for
each executive challenge.

\section{Interaction Design Principles}

The neurodiversity framework above establishes what a strength-based tool should
be grounded in; interaction design principles determine how to make that
grounding real and effective in practice. Well-chosen design approaches can
amplify the neurodiversity framework---not only translating each strength--deficit
pairing into a usable interaction, but actively reshaping the way people with
ADHD perceive and experience their own capabilities.

The inherent mental model of people with ADHD is largely negative---``I need to
be managed’’---a framing reinforced by existing deficit-oriented
tools~\cite{harpin2016}. This raises the question of how a tool might instead
reshape that model with positive elements. To explore this, we examined existing
research on mental models and mobile interface design. Mental
models---abstract cognitive structures through which users interpret and predict
an application~\cite{rook1993}---are central to user-centred
design~\cite{ruiz2021}. Research shows that visual, symbolic, and pictorial
design elements are the primary medium through which mental models are
conceptualised~\cite{goldschmidt2007,ourusoff1985}, and that structuring
information around action-relevant schemas supports effective retrieval and
application~\cite{ruggles1998}. A systematic review of 257 widely cited UI
design principles identifies six core guidelines: providing informative
feedback, minimising memory load, clear error messages, providing shortcuts,
using natural language, and using real-world metaphors~\cite{ruiz2021}. These
findings confirm that a user’s mental model can be actively reshaped through
design. Grounded in the neurodiversity framework above and guided by these
principles, we proceeded to design a strength-based task management tool.

\section{Design}

The design process proceeded in three stages. First, the six strength--deficit
neural pairings established in Section~2.1 were each translated into an abstract
design principle, forming a theoretical framework.
Second, each principle was instantiated as a concrete interaction module,
producing six distinct engagement mechanisms. Third, these modules were composed
into a unified workflow and a consistent visual design system, with an aesthetic
language chosen to itself reinforce the strength-based mental model. The
resulting application was named \textbf{StrengthFlow}---reflecting the core
design intent: to channel each cognitive strength into structured, purposeful
task engagement. The subsections below describe each stage in detail.

\subsection{Module Logic}

StrengthFlow implements one interaction module per strength--deficit pairing.
Each module is described across five dimensions: challenge, cognitive strength,
design principle, implementation, and intended outcome.

\textbf{Concentration Module} \textit{(Cognitive Vitality — Concentration).}
\textbf{Challenge:} Difficulty sustaining attention on external tasks due to persistent internal ideation.
\textbf{Strength:} Spontaneous, non-sequential divergent thinking.
\textbf{Principle:} Use divergent thinking as an entry point for redirecting attention toward externally required tasks.
\textbf{Implementation:} The user flips five randomly selected image cards, each concealing a word, and must use all five revealed terms as the basis for a creative piece whose subject is their current external task---connecting internal ideation directly to the required output.
\textbf{Intended outcome:} Creative engagement bridges intrinsic ideation and the extrinsic task, redirecting rather than suppressing internal cognitive energy.

\textbf{Memory Module} \textit{(Transcendence — Memory).}
\textbf{Challenge:} Difficulty retaining task-relevant information in working memory.
\textbf{Strength:} Deep aesthetic absorption facilitating encoding during experiences of wonder or beauty.
\textbf{Principle:} Use aesthetically meaningful representations of task information to support encoding and retention.
\textbf{Implementation:} Before a puzzle activity, the user enters the task items to remember; each is assigned to a puzzle piece. Hovering over a piece displays its corresponding information---reinforcing encoding through aesthetic interaction rather than rote repetition.
\textbf{Intended outcome:} Aesthetic engagement during entry and recall supports retention without reliance on external reminders.

\textbf{Persistent Tasks Module} \textit{(Humanity — Execution).}
\textbf{Challenge:} Difficulty sustaining recurring responsibilities and adapting when routines are interrupted.
\textbf{Strength:} Empathy, social connectedness, and a sense of responsibility towards others.
\textbf{Principle:} Use relational meaning and social interaction to sustain engagement during routine task execution.
\textbf{Implementation:} Recurring responsibilities are represented as planets in a personal solar-system map, each linked to a person or valued commitment. Completing a task illuminates the corresponding planet; users may optionally share progress or exchange supportive messages with others.
\textbf{Intended outcome:} Connecting routine tasks to valued relationships makes persistent responsibilities feel meaningful and supports continued execution.

\textbf{Long-term Tasks Module} \textit{(Courage — Emotion).}
\textbf{Challenge:} Aversive responses to long-term reward anticipation, producing impulsivity and procrastination.
\textbf{Strength:} Responsiveness to immediate positive stimuli and risk-taking under short-horizon conditions.
\textbf{Principle:} Support engagement through positively framed, short-horizon actions that replace aversive distant outcomes.
\textbf{Implementation:} Via an Instant Rewards mechanism, each long-term task is broken into short-term sub-tasks, each linked to an immediate positive reward upon completion---replacing the aversive full-task horizon with a series of achievable steps.
\textbf{Intended outcome:} Short-horizon reward cycles reduce procrastination by replacing aversive delay with immediate, achievable goals.

\textbf{Priority Tasks Module} \textit{(Energy — Activation).}
\textbf{Challenge:} Difficulty initiating tasks that lack personal significance or a meaningful activation stimulus.
\textbf{Strength:} Willpower motivated by personally meaningful goals; hyper-focus when an ambitious goal is present.
\textbf{Principle:} Connect tasks to personally meaningful goals and higher-order purposes to support initiation.
\textbf{Implementation:} The user selects a role or persona from a favourite film or television production and completes priority tasks within that imaginative scenario---anchoring execution in personal meaning rather than abstract obligation.
\textbf{Intended outcome:} Embedding tasks within a meaningful narrative activates goal-directed energy without relying on external authority.

\textbf{Emotion Module} \textit{(Resilience — Effort).}
\textbf{Challenge:} Difficulty sustaining self-regulation over time; mental fatigue under cognitive load.
\textbf{Strength:} Capacity for self-regulation and behavioural adjustment under adverse conditions.
\textbf{Principle:} Offer adaptive coping strategies as flexible, optional resources during cognitive fatigue.
\textbf{Implementation:} Mood-contingent branching: in a positive state, the user expresses themselves through social connection---helping others, sharing feedback, or engaging with the community; in a negative state, positive-thinking resources such as meditation and guided reflection are offered.
\textbf{Intended outcome:} Mood-contingent branching supports coping without imposing a fixed sequence, preserving agency during ego depletion.

\subsection{Workflow}

The application workflow follows a non-linear model: users enter whichever
module matches their current cognitive state rather than following a prescribed
sequence. This respects the temporal experience of ADHD, in which time is
perceived as a dispersed collection of events linked to people and emotions
rather than a sequential schedule. As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:workflow},
the entry point is a unified home screen from which users can freely navigate
to any of the six strength-based modules. Transitions between modules require
no fixed order; users may switch at any point in response to their current
cognitive or emotional state. This architecture avoids imposing a linear task
queue---a design choice grounded in research showing that rigid sequential
workflows increase cognitive load and reduce engagement for adults with
ADHD~\cite{safren2006}.

\subsection{Visual Design Principles}

People with ADHD have heightened visuospatial processing sensitivity. The
StrengthFlow interface therefore uses: non-repetitive, non-patterned layouts;
vivid colours
associated with high energy and positivity; irregular icon shapes that echo
creativity and divergent thinking; and aesthetically distinctive artwork within
task modules. The UI adheres to six empirically validated core principles:
informative feedback, minimal memory load, clear error messages, shortcuts,
natural language, and real-world metaphors~\cite{ruiz2021}.

\section{Prototype}

A high-fidelity prototype was developed from a wireframe framework that
implements the module logic described above. The visual system consistently
applies the design principles across all modules, ensuring that aesthetic
stimulation supports---rather than distracts from---each cognitive strength
activation. The prototype is interactive and supports the full task-entry-to-
completion flow for each module.

\section{User Testing}

Preliminary evaluation was conducted with three anonymous adults with ADHD.
Positive feedback: the application is engaging and the colour palette
successfully attracts and holds attention. Critical finding: in noisy external
environments (e.g.\ commuting), ambient auditory distractions competed with the
visual engagement (see Figure~\ref{fig:phone}). As a result, the next iteration of StrengthFlow will integrate background
music and sound effects. Prior research shows that neurofeedback audio
protocols are a well-established adjunctive treatment for ADHD~\cite{enriquez2019},
providing a principled basis for this extension.

\section{Conclusion}

StrengthFlow is, to our knowledge, the first mobile task-management application
grounded in a strength-based neurodiversity framework for ADHD. Rather than
relying on reminders, timers, or behavioural correction, StrengthFlow positions
each cognitive strength as a conceptual basis for an interaction module that
addresses the corresponding executive challenge. The underlying design approach is replicable:
identify a cognitive strength and its associated executive challenge in the
literature, derive an abstract design principle from that pairing, and implement
a concrete interaction that instantiates the principle without imposing
deficit-oriented constraints.

Limitations include a small user testing sample (N\,=\,3) and limited coverage of
users with deafblindness. Future work will expand evaluation and incorporate
neurofeedback technology as wireless EEG device costs continue to fall. Beyond task management, StrengthFlow aims to shift the mental model of ADHD
from ``I need to be managed'' to one that affirms capability and positive
identity.
