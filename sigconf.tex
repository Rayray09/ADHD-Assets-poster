\begin{abstract}
ADHD is commonly framed through hyperactivity, impulsivity, inattention, and executive-function difficulties, while its associated cognitive strengths receive less attention. Existing task management applications for people with ADHD often rely on reminders, timers, and behavioural correction, potentially reinforcing deficit-orientated assumptions.

This paper presents StrengthFlow, a strength-based task management application for adults with ADHD. It translates six ADHD-related cognitive strengths into design principles addressing challenges in activation, concentration, effort, emotion, memory, and task execution. A high-fidelity prototype was preliminarily evaluated with three adults with ADHD. Feedback indicated positive responses to its strength-based framing and visual design, while highlighting the need to better support use in distracting environments. StrengthFlow demonstrates how neurodiversity-informed design can promote capability, autonomy, and positive identity, reflecting the principle that “people with differences may not need a cure; they need help and accommodation.”

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
ADHD is commonly understood through deficits in attention and executive function. For adults with ADHD, executive dysfunction is a central challenge\cite{nigg2005}, affecting the cognitive processes that support flexible, goal-directed behaviour and everyday task completion~\cite{miyake2000}. However, ADHD characteristics cannot be reduced to a binary distinction between ``normal'' and ``abnormal''~\cite{sedgwick2019}. Adults with ADHD may experience difficulties in cognitive control, reward sensitivity, and time perception, while also
displaying creativity, high energy, hyperfocus, empathy, and a willingness to help others. These coexisting characteristics suggest that ADHD support should not only address difficulties, but also recognise and activate individual strengths\cite{sedgwick2019}. Yet few tools currently treat ADHD-related strengths as resources for supporting executive function.

Digital task-management applications are increasingly used as complementary psychosocial support for adults with ADHD, although evidence of their sustained effectiveness remains limited~\cite{pasarelu2020}. More importantly, many existing tools rely on reminders, monitoring, and external behavioural
control. While these mechanisms may support task completion, they can also reinforce a deficit-oriented mental model in which users feel that 'I need to be managed', undermining self-esteem\cite{torous2016}~\cite{gajecki2014}.

A key assumption of the neurodiversity paradigm is that neurological
diversity is valuable and should be respected as a natural form of human
variation~\cite{denhouting2019}. Grounded in this perspective, this poster presents \textit{StrengthFlow}, a strength-oriented mobile task-management concept grounded in neurodiversity. It pairs the six dimensions of ADHD executive-function difficulty proposed by
Brown\cite{nigg2005}, activation, concentration, effort, emotion, memory, and execution; with the six positive ADHD-related characteristics identified by Schippers et al.~\cite{sedgwick2019}, energy, cognitive vitality, resilience, courage, transcendence, and humanity~\cite{schippers2022}. StrengthFlow translates each pairing into an interactive task module. Rather than suppressing ADHD the deficit of people with ADHD, StrengthFlow recognises that their effects are context-dependent and explores how they can be redirected as resources for task engagement, user autonomy, and a more positive understanding of ADHD.


\section{User Needs and Gaps in Existing ADHD Tools}

Approximately 2.8\% of adults worldwide have ADHD~\cite{faraone2006}.
Studies involving 422 adults identified strengths including problem-solving, deep focus, empathy, and resilience, but limited external recognition
~\cite{sedgwick2019,schippers2022}. Meanwhile, 29--56\% reported low
self-esteem and 38--73\% reported impaired social functioning
~\cite{harpin2016}. These findings highlight the need to affirm ADHD strengths while providing positive, non-coercive support.

Existing ADHD applications remain largely deficit-oriented, with limited
evidence of effectiveness~\cite{pasarelu2020}. Our exploratory analysis of 22 applications found that most relied on reminders, timers, and behavioural prompts, while strength-aligned features remained fragmented
(Table~\ref{tab:apps}). This gap motivated \textit{StrengthFlow}.

\section{Neurodiversity Framework: Strengths and Executive Challenges}

The neurodiversity of executive function in ADHD involves four interconnected
brain regions: the prefrontal cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum, and parietal-temporal regions, operating through the reticular activating system~\cite{castellanos2002,valera2007,nakao2011,durston2004}. These regions
do not function independently, but form a dynamic and integrated system. The same underlying processes may produce different outcomes across contexts,
contributing to an executive difficulty in one situation while supporting a
cognitive strength in another. Based on this perspective, this section examines the connections between six dimensions of executive-function difficulty and six positive ADHD-related themes, and explores whether these pairings can provide a logical framework
for mobile application modules.

First, \textbf{Concentration Difficulty and Cognitive Vitality} in ADHD are
associated with internally directed mental activity and the default mode
network~\cite{ammaniti2013,stinemorrow2008,sestieri2011}. Continued internal
thought can disrupt sustained attention to external tasks; however, it can also
support vivid imagery, rapid associations, curiosity, creativity, and divergent
thinking~\cite{mole2022}. Second, \textbf{Emotional Dysregulation and Courage}
are associated with emotional and reward processing in the amygdala and ventral
striatum~\cite{williams2008,plessen2006,plichta2014}. Heightened emotional
reactivity and reduced responsiveness to delayed rewards can contribute to
impulsive decisions and procrastination; however, responsiveness to immediate
or personally meaningful outcomes can support rapid action, risk-taking, and
persistence~\cite{du2006,knutson2001}. Third, \textbf{Activation Difficulty
and Energy} are associated with the balance between goal-directed and
stimulus-driven attention~\cite{corbetta2002}. Difficulty shifting attention
can delay task initiation, prioritisation, and switching; however, personally
meaningful goals can generate strong motivation, sustained engagement, and
hyperfocus~\cite{schippers2022}. Fourth, \textbf{Behavioural Adaptation
Difficulty and Humanity} are associated with social-cognitive and emotional
processes involved in recognising and responding to others
~\cite{humphrey1976,salovey1990}. Difficulty interpreting changing social cues
can hinder behavioural adjustment; however, sensitivity to others can support
empathy, humour, positive emotional expression, and social connection. Fifth,
\textbf{Sustained-Effort Difficulty and Resilience} are associated with
conflict monitoring and behavioural regulation involving the anterior
cingulate cortex~\cite{garmezy1971,carter2007}. Prolonged cognitive control can
cause mental fatigue and reduce sustained effort; however, the same regulatory
processes can support coping, self-regulation, and behavioural adaptation
~\cite{baumeister2000}. Finally, \textbf{Working-Memory Difficulty and
Transcendence} are associated with the processing of emotionally,
aesthetically, and personally salient experiences. Ordinary or low-salience
task information can be difficult to retain and retrieve; however, meaningful
experiences can promote deep absorption and stronger memory encoding
~\cite{martinez2016,shiota2006}.

Together, these relationships form six ADHD strength-challenge pairings:
cognitive vitality-concentration, courage-emotion, energy-activation,
humanity-execution, resilience-effort, and transcendence-memory. These
pairings provide the conceptual basis for the six interaction modules
developed in Section~5.

\section{Design}
Building on the six strength--challenge pairings in Section~3, StrengthFlow translates each into a design principle and interaction module within a unified non-linear workflow and visual system.
\subsection{Interaction Design Principles}

StrengthFlow aims to replace the deficit-oriented mental model of
``I need to be managed'' with one that emphasises capability and autonomy.
Mental models shape how users interpret and interact with digital systems
~\cite{rook1993,ruggles1998}, while visual, symbolic, and pictorial elements
can influence how these models are formed~\cite{goldschmidt2007,ourusoff1985}.

The interface therefore follows six widely cited interaction design
principles: providing informative feedback, minimising memory load, offering
clear error messages, providing shortcuts, using natural language, and
employing real-world metaphors~\cite{ruiz2021}. These principles are applied
across all six modules to make the strength-based framework clear, usable, and
consistent.

\subsection{Module Logic}

StrengthFlow translates the six strength--challenge pairings into six
interaction modules, each applying a distinct design principle. First, the
\textbf{Concentration Module} uses cognitive vitality to support concentration
by asking users to connect five random words to their current task, redirecting
divergent thinking towards external work. Second, the \textbf{Long-term Task
Module} uses courage to support emotional engagement by replacing a distant
outcome with achievable, positively framed steps and immediate rewards. Third,
the \textbf{Priority Task Module} uses energy to support task activation by
embedding a priority task within a personally meaningful role or narrative.
Fourth, the \textbf{Persistent Task Module} uses humanity to support task
execution by representing recurring responsibilities as planets linked to
valued people or commitments. Fifth, the \textbf{Adaptive Coping Module} uses
resilience to sustain effort by offering flexible social or reflective
activities based on the user's current state. Finally, the \textbf{Memory
Module} uses transcendence to support memory by linking task information to an
aesthetic puzzle that encourages encoding and recall. The complete module
logic is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:module-logic}.

\subsection{Workflow}

The workflow is non-linear: from a unified home screen, users can enter or
switch between any of the six modules according to their current cognitive or
emotional state (Figure~\ref{fig:workflow}). This avoids imposing a fixed task
sequence and better accommodates the non-sequential temporal experience often
reported by adults with ADHD~\cite{safren2006}.

\section{Prototype and Visual Implementation}

A low-fidelity wireframe was first developed to define the structure,
navigation, and task flow of the six modules. It was then developed into an
interactive high-fidelity prototype using vivid colours, irregular shapes,
non-repetitive layouts, and distinctive artwork to reflect the energy,
creativity, and divergent thinking associated with ADHD. These visual elements
are applied consistently across the application to reinforce its
strength-based framing while maintaining clear navigation and task
information~\cite{ruiz2021}. The prototype supports the complete
task-entry-to-completion flow for each module, as shown in
Figures~\ref{fig:prototype} and~\ref{fig:phone}.

\section{User Testing}

Formative testing was conducted with three adults with ADHD. Participants
described StrengthFlow as engaging and found that its colour palette attracted
their attention. However, they also reported that environmental noise,
particularly while commuting, could compete with the visually focused
interaction. Based on this feedback, future iterations will explore optional,
user-controlled audio cues or soundscapes and evaluate whether they support
engagement in distracting environments without introducing additional
sensory load.



\section{Conclusion}

StrengthFlow is, to our knowledge, the first mobile task-management application
grounded in a strength-based neurodiversity framework for ADHD. Rather than
relying on reminders, timers, or behavioural correction, StrengthFlow positions
each cognitive strength as a conceptual basis for an interaction module that
addresses the corresponding executive challenge. The underlying design approach is replicable:
identify a cognitive strength and its associated executive challenge in the
literature, derive an abstract design principle from that pairing, and implement
a concrete interaction that instantiates the principle without imposing
deficit-oriented constraints.

Limitations include a small user testing sample (N\,=\,3) and limited coverage of
users with deafblindness. Future work will expand evaluation and incorporate
neurofeedback technology as wireless EEG device costs continue to fall. Beyond task management, StrengthFlow aims to shift the mental model of ADHD
from ``I need to be managed'' to one that affirms capability and positive
identity.
